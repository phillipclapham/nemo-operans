Civil War Is Their Win Condition

Why Not Taking the Bait Is Strategic, Not Surrender

January 2026

On January 24th, 2026, federal agents shot and killed Alex Pretti in Minneapolis.

He was 37. An ICU nurse at a VA hospital. A legal gun owner with a valid concealed carry permit and no criminal record.

The video is unambiguous: Pretti was holding his phone, checking on a woman who'd been pushed to the ground by agents. They tackled him, secured his holstered weapon, and then—after disarming him—shot him approximately ten times.

Within hours, Stephen Miller called him a "domestic terrorist" who "tried to assassinate federal law enforcement."

The Washington Post, Reuters, BBC, and New York Times all independently verified what the video shows: he never drew a weapon. He was holding a phone.

They're not even trying to make the lie believable anymore.

That's not incompetence. That's a dare.

Then something interesting happened.

Trump personally intervened. He removed the Border Patrol commander running the operation. He talked to the Minneapolis mayor and promised de-escalation. He sent his border czar Tom Homan to take over—reporting "directly to me," bypassing his own DHS Secretary.

The surface changed.

But watch who didn't walk it back.

Vice President Vance continued calling the protests "engineered chaos" by "far-left agitators." He reposted Miller's claim that Pretti was an assassin. Homeland Security Secretary Noem repeated the lie that Pretti was "brandishing" a weapon—contradicted by every independent video analysis.

The policy architects kept pushing the provocation while the figurehead pulled back.

Why?

Because they're operating under different constraints.

Trump needs legitimacy. He needs his coalition—including the gun owners who suddenly noticed that a lawfully-armed American was killed by federal agents for being lawfully armed. The NRA called the administration's justifications "dangerous and wrong." Republican Representative Thomas Massie said "carrying a firearm is not a death sentence." Conservative radio host Dana Loesch wrote that anyone defending the shooting is "an anti-2A statist."

For the first time, MAGA's ideology collided with the reality of federal enforcement. And Trump felt the fracture.

The people running the infrastructure don't have this constraint. The surveillance contracts doubled to nearly a billion dollars this year. The AI tracking systems are being deployed. The enforcement technology gets built whether Trump de-escalates or not.

The surface responds to political pressure. The infrastructure doesn't care about optics.

Here's what I need you to understand: civil war is their win condition, not ours.

Not "both sides lose." Not "mutually assured destruction."

They win. We lose. The math is that simple.

Think about what a fragmented, violent America actually looks like. Neighbor against neighbor. Region against region. Trust shattered. Institutions delegitimized. Normal governance impossible.

Now think about who benefits from that scenario.

A united population turning its attention to the 1% would be genuinely dangerous to them. That's not what's forming. What's forming is exactly the fragmentation that lets a small class retreat to their compounds while the rest of us bleed.

This isn't speculation. They've told us. In 2009, Peter Thiel wrote an essay with a line that deserves more attention: "I no longer believe that freedom and democracy are compatible."

He argued that technology should outpace democracy's ability to regulate it, creating a de facto aristocracy of those who control technological infrastructure.

Fifteen years later, his protégé is Vice President. His companies have nearly a billion dollars in federal surveillance contracts. More than thirty people from his network hold federal positions.

But here's the thing: I'm not claiming Thiel is "the architect." He might be one articulator of a sentiment that runs through an entire class. What matters isn't the individual—it's the pattern.

There's a class of people who genuinely believe democracy is incompatible with their vision of freedom. Thiel wrote it down. Others may believe it without publishing essays. The ideology is the threat, not any single person.

And the ideology has a strategy: make democratic governance impossible, then offer technological order as the replacement.

Civil war serves this perfectly. Not because they want violence for its own sake—but because fragmentation creates the conditions for their solution.

Every provocation is strategic.

When Stephen Miller called Alex Pretti a "domestic terrorist" within hours of video evidence showing otherwise, he wasn't making a mistake. He wasn't failing at propaganda.

He was daring you to get angry.

The gaslighting isn't meant to convince you. It's meant to enrage you.

Think about what happens when you see an obvious lie repeated by powerful people with zero consequences. The natural response is outrage. Protest. Maybe, eventually, violence.

And what do they get from that?

They get footage. They get "violent leftists" that justify the crackdown they're already building legal frameworks for. They get further polarization that makes their opponents look dangerous while they position themselves as restoring order.

The obvious lie is a dare. Every time you take the bait—every time righteous anger spills into the reaction they're engineering—you're playing their game.

This is why the Pretti response matters so much. The lie was SO obvious, the video SO clear, that the provocation was barely disguised. They're not even pretending anymore.

Because they don't need to. The trap works whether you see it or not.

So what do we do?

I'm not going to pretend this is satisfying. The natural human response to injustice is action. When you watch federal agents kill a nurse for holding a phone and then call him a terrorist, "don't get too angry" feels like capitulation.

It isn't.

Don't take the bait is not the same as do nothing. It's refusing to play a game where every outcome favors the house.

Violence at this stage—any violence that can be framed as "leftist"—mathematically favors the people building the crackdown infrastructure. It gives them exactly what they need. It fragments the opposition. It justifies acceleration.

The people who want you enraged are not stupid. They understand that a united, disciplined opposition is dangerous to them. They understand that an opposition that can be provoked into scattered violence is manageable.

Every provocation is designed to trigger the manageable response.

Not taking the bait means:

Seeing the game for what it is. Understanding that the obvious lie is a dare, not a failure. Recognizing that your anger—however justified—is the resource they're trying to extract.

It means directing energy toward what actually threatens them: organization, coalition-building, the long patient work of creating alternatives. Not the cathartic release they're engineering you toward.

I know. It's not satisfying. But the satisfying response is the one they're counting on.

Here's what gives me hope: they're not monolithic.

The Pretti shooting revealed something important. When Trump personally intervened to de-escalate while his own Vice President continued the provocation, we saw the fault line.

Trump needs voters. His coalition includes gun owners who suddenly realized federal enforcement could happen to them. When the NRA and conservative radio hosts started pushing back, Trump felt the political cost.

The infrastructure people don't have this constraint. They profit whether Trump maintains legitimacy or not. Their surveillance contracts, their AI systems, their enforcement technology—none of it depends on winning elections.

This creates a tension. And tensions crack.

Remember when Musk and Trump had their spectacular falling out? The man who contributed $260 million, who had near-daily White House presence, who was restructuring the federal government—within months was calling Trump's bill "disgusting," invoking Epstein, threatening to form a rival party.

They reconciled. For now.

But the crack revealed something fundamental: these are not people capable of subordinating ego to collective purpose. The very traits that made them billionaires—relentless self-assertion, conviction they're the smartest in any room—make genuine cooperation impossible over the long term.

They can align temporarily. They can coordinate against common enemies. But the moment interests diverge, they turn on each other.

Their victory looks assured. It isn't.

The leopard always believes it will only eat other people's faces.

The surface shifted this week in Minneapolis. The infrastructure didn't.

That's the distinction that matters. That's what this analysis is for.

I'm not asking you to feel hopeful. I'm not asking you to feel anything in particular. Emotions are resources in this game, and I'm not trying to manipulate yours.

I'm asking you to see.

Civil war is their win condition. Every provocation is strategic. The obvious lie is a dare. They're not monolithic, and they will eventually eat each other.

What we do with that understanding is up to us. But it starts with refusing to play a game rigged for our loss.

Don't take the bait.

Not because it's satisfying. Because it's strategic.

The cracks are there. The contradictions are real. Not taking the bait isn't about waiting them out—it's about denying them the common enemy that papers over their fractures.
